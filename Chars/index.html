Pakistan's Security Crisis Analysis

An In-Depth Review of Recent Events & Their Implications

LIVE AND LET OTHER LIVE

Made By CADET ZAIN Quoted by SYED MUZAMMIL SHAH

The biggest problem facing Pakistan — which has always threatened the states image, its revenue, the mental health of its citizens, their safety, the safety of their children, their businesses, their wealth, their investments, and overall public life — is terrorism. A major wave of it has been witnessed in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa since the Taliban regime came to power. But it is not limited to there; we have also seen multiple incidents of terrorism in Balochistan. The recent incident that occurred two days ago, carried out by the Baloch Liberation Army (BLA), was, in my view, the largest coordinated attack in recent times.

If you consider the Jaffar Express attack of 2024 or the suicide blast at the railway station, none of those incidents involved such a large-scale coordinated assault by militants as what we saw on January 31. How did this attack happen? What were BLA’s objectives? Which places, locations, and people were targeted? How much damage did the state suffer? How much loss did the terrorists incur? What are the implications? Is there foreign involvement or not? And what is the solution? Let’s discuss this today.

On January 31 at around 3 AM, the BLA formally began its operations, targeting nine districts and twelve locations. This meant that nearly 30% of Balochistan’s districts came under attack. One after another, even Quetta city — the provincial capital — witnessed militants openly moving around. Whether it was Sariab Road, the Hazara Ganji area, or the Khaliq Shaheed Police Station, which was destroyed — clashes were visible everywhere. Gunfire and explosions were heard at Imdad Chowk. Sheikh Zayed Hospital was stormed in an attempt to take control, and staff at Civil Hospital Quetta were targeted.

Tactically, the BLA also attempted to block major highways connecting different areas of Balochistan. They set up checkpoints on:

  • N-25 (Quetta to Karachi)
  • N-40 (towards Taftan)
  • N-65 (towards Sibi)

They used these checkpoints to disconnect the province. The Western Bypass was used for transportation purposes. In Pasni and Gwadar, passengers were taken off buses and attempts were made to abduct them. Importantly, these were not isolated weekly incidents — they occurred simultaneously in a coordinated manner.

Attacks were also carried out in Mastung city and Dasht. The DC complex, Levies control room, and Tehsil office in Dasht were attacked. In Nushki, the Deputy Commissioner, Muhammad Hussain, was abducted. A video surfaced showing him stating he was in BLA custody. Later, Chief Minister Sarfraz Bugti said he was in a safe location, though his exact whereabouts remain unconfirmed. Attempts were also made to storm the FC headquarters in Nushki, though unsuccessfully. There were plans or attempts to target the Judicial Complex, Cadet College, SP House, and Assistant Commissioner’s residence.

One of the most unfortunate aspects was targeting civilian soft targets. In Gwadar, labor colonies were attacked, and reports say eleven workers were killed. These were ordinary laborers from outside the province.

In Dalbandin, grenades were thrown at government offices. Firing was reported in Pasni, Harnai, Kalat, Chagai, and Nokkundi. Attempts were made to blow up railway tracks, including at Rabi Bridge in Nasirabad, as reported by The Guardian.

International media reported that banks were looted in Quetta (Hazara Ganji), Mastung, and Kalat. Videos showed attacks on Faisal Bank and other banks, where grenades and rocket launchers were used to break walls and steal cash. Reports claim approximately 3.5 million rupees were stolen from National Bank of Pakistan and HBL branches.

Official death toll figures vary:

  • The government claims 185 militants were killed.
  • It says it has custody of 145 militant bodies.
  • The BLA claims it killed 85 army personnel.
  • The Pakistani government states around 17 security personnel were killed and 18 unarmed civilians were targeted.

Interior Minister Mohsin Naqvi and Chief Minister Sarfraz Bugti directly blamed India, alleging it provides assistance to the BLA. Section 144 was imposed, banning public gatherings. There were also claims that female suicide bombers were used in coordinated strikes.

The operation was named “Herof 2” by BLA spokesperson Jeeyand Baloch. A video circulated allegedly showing BLA commander Bashir Zaib riding a motorcycle freely in mountainous terrain. The government suggested it could be footage from Afghanistan, as it would be difficult for him to move freely inside Pakistan under heavy surveillance.

For the citizens of Quetta and Balochistan, this was devastating. Militants were roaming openly, banks were looted, and people were terrorized. In response, heavy security measures were imposed: checkpoints increased, internet disruptions occurred, surveillance intensified. The local population suffers immensely — business losses, children’s safety at risk, and long-term psychological trauma. Many people in Quetta say the memory of explosions has haunted them since childhood. Everyday life has become deeply traumatic.

Regarding BLA’s structure, it has multiple factions: BLA Jeeyand, BLA Azad, BLF, BRA, BRAS, Majeed Brigade, etc. The Majeed Brigade has dedicated suicide units and claims inspiration from Kurdish movements.

There are also alleged alliances between BLA and TTP (Tehreek-e-Taliban Pakistan). UN monitoring reports (2025) reportedly identified shared training sites in South Afghanistan, including Wali Kot and Shorabak, where BLA and TTP fighters allegedly trained together. Arrested Taliban leaders allegedly admitted operational coordination with Bashir Zaib.

Since the Taliban returned to power in Afghanistan, terrorism has reportedly increased. Data suggests a 14% rise in terrorism in 2025 compared to the previous year, with 1,035 Pakistanis killed and 700 terrorist incidents recorded.

The argument presented is philosophical: grievances in Balochistan — poverty, lack of schools, water issues, resource distribution — are real and legitimate. However, using violence against civilians, looting banks, targeting labor camps, and attacking soft targets does not constitute a “just war.”

Referencing Thomas Aquinas’ Just War Theory:

  1. Legitimate authority — Does BLA represent all Baloch people? No clear evidence.
  2. Just cause — Grievances may be legitimate.
  3. Right intention — Avoidance of evil and protection of civilians? Not evident.

Thus, even if grievances are real, the violent methods fail the moral test of a just war.

The proposed solution includes:

  • Establishing a National Truth and Reconciliation Commission.
  • Addressing missing persons allegations transparently.
  • Fair revenue sharing and resource justice.
  • Economic inclusion and community policing.
  • Strengthening the Senate to protect smaller provinces’ rights.
  • Avoiding labeling everyone a terrorist.
  • Ending the information blackout.
  • Regional engagement and international coordination regarding cross-border militancy.

The argument concludes that purely military (kinetic) operations will not solve the issue. A political process, trust-building, and reconciliation are necessary. Let Baloch leadership genuinely represent Baloch people through fair elections. Only a visionary, not reactive, approach can bring long-term peace.
